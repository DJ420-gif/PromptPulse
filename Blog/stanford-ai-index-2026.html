The Numbers That Define 2026

Stanford's Institute for Human-Centered AI released the 2026 AI Index today — a 400+ page annual report on the state of AI. The defining claim: AI adoption is outpacing both the personal computer and the internet in speed of uptake. AI companies are generating revenue faster than any previous technology boom while simultaneously spending hundreds of billions on data centres and chips. $242 billion was invested in AI globally in Q1 2026 alone — 80% of all global venture capital for the quarter. Four of the five largest venture rounds ever recorded closed in Q1 2026. The report positions 2026 as the year AI stops being a technology category and becomes infrastructure — as foundational as electricity or internet connectivity.

02

Humanity's Last Exam — From 8.8% to 50% in 18 Months

The benchmark story is the most striking in the report. Humanity's Last Exam — questions from subject matter experts representing the hardest problems in their fields, designed to be impossible to memorise — saw top AI scoring 8.8% in early 2025. By April 2026, Claude Opus 4.6 and Gemini 3.1 Pro are scoring above 50%. That is a 5x improvement in 18 months on a benchmark specifically designed to resist AI capability. The Stanford researchers are careful to note this does not necessarily map to real-world performance — 75% on a legal reasoning benchmark tells you little about fit for an actual law firm workflow. But the trajectory makes clear no capability ceiling is in sight.

03

US vs China — Near Parity With Different Advantages

The competitive landscape data is detailed and important. The US leads on frontier model performance (though the gap is narrow), capital deployed, and compute infrastructure — 5,427 data centres, more than 10 times any other country. China leads on AI research publications, patents and robotics deployment. The report explicitly notes that leading AI companies no longer disclose training code, parameter counts or dataset sizes — a transparency regression from 2023. As of April 2026, Anthropic leads on model performance rankings, trailed closely by xAI, Google and OpenAI, with Chinese models like DeepSeek and Alibaba lagging only modestly. The competitive window between US and Chinese frontier models has narrowed to razor-thin margins — the race is now on cost, reliability and real-world usefulness rather than raw benchmark numbers.

04

AI and Jobs — The Honest Data

The workforce numbers are more nuanced than headlines suggest. AI is boosting productivity by 14% in customer service and 26% in software development. A third of organisations expect AI to shrink their workforce in service, supply chain and software engineering. But the report is careful: it distinguishes between automation of specific tasks and elimination of entire roles — and notes the gains are not seen in tasks requiring judgment, creativity or interpersonal nuance. The report's most honest framing: 59% of people think AI will provide more benefits than drawbacks, while 52% say it makes them nervous. Both things are simultaneously true, and the economic impact remains genuinely too early to measure confidently despite what both AI optimists and doomsayers claim.
